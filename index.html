Cover Letter
TARGET: Enterprise Customer Success · Technical Account Management · Strategic Accounts

Dear Hiring Manager,

I wrote my first automation script in 1999, at eight years old, for a game called EverQuest. I never stopped building. But my career took shape somewhere more valuable: the seam between deep technical work and the customer across the table.

I studied Computer Science & Information Systems at UVU; an auto-auction job paid for school. Ten years later I was its General Manager, refactoring the entire Java-based auction stack and running the website while still working the floor — hundreds of cars a week. Sales grew year over year for a decade, on software I rebuilt, campaigns I created, and inventory I fought for: new dealer and bank accounts, even working with local law enforcement on repossessions. And I sold cars myself — sell someone one car, and they brought their entire family back. That is what trust is — not a pitch, but accountability to the same people week after week. We called it customers for life.

At Black Box Code I became the engineering side’s guardian. I absorbed complex client architectures fast and jumped in front of the train so architects and developers could stay focused on scope, TDD, and clean delivery. At SMPL Finance I ran enterprise complexity as Senior Project Manager: procurement, resourcing, shifting scope, clear reports for decision-makers. I know how it feels to deliver on a deadline because the campaign is already live and the runway is finite. When I sit with a customer’s project owners, I am not sympathizing. I have been them.

In 2023 I bet on myself and went all-in on the AI shift, building my own coding agents and task-management systems from the ground up at Sentry Mandate — full days, most nights, for three years. Those dreams will live on happily in my garage. What I want now is a good home: a team where all of that building goes into helping other companies get the future right.

That is my offer: the face of the company, carried by someone who has owned the P&L, engineered the stack, and shipped the product — at home with complicated specs, regulated data, and high-security accounts. The relationship is the part I have practiced my whole life.

Thank you for your time and consideration.

Sincerely,
Jared F. Helm

Leadership Narrative
The Seam I Live On

Most careers pick a side: technical or customer-facing. Mine never did. Every chapter — auction floor, engineering shop, finance PMO, AI lab — has been both at once. That seam is where enterprise accounts are won and kept.

Why Customers Trust Me With Their Accounts

Trust at a live auction is earned in public: same floor, same faces, week after week, with money on the line. Ten years of that builds a reflex — never let the person across the table be surprised. As the face of a company, I bring bad news early and with a plan, translate the roadmap into their outcomes, and stay accountable long after the signature.

  • Face-of-the-company presence, earned on live auction floors
  • Owner’s empathy — I know what runway and payroll feel like
  • Escalation shield: bad news early, always with a plan
  • Fluent in complex specs, architectures, and security postures
  • PM instincts — deadlines exist because campaigns and budgets do
  • Builder: I can prototype the fix, not just file the ticket
How I Run an Account

I do not win by sounding technical, and I do not win by charm. I win by making the customer’s success legible to both sides — their executives and our engineers — until delivery, renewal, and expansion stop being separate conversations.

LISTEN
Learn what they are actually carrying. Not the org-chart version — revenue targets, campaign dates, budget runway, and the people whose jobs depend on delivery.
TRANSLATE
Bridge the two languages. Technical specs into business outcomes for their executives; business pressure into precise, respectful asks for our engineers.
DELIVER
Make trust compound. Program reviews, delivery cadence, honest status, early escalation, and proof — so every renewal conversation starts from evidence, not persuasion.
Operating Thesis

Give me the account everyone else calls difficult — the tangled stack, the shifting scope, the stakeholders who have stopped believing. I will learn their business, earn a seat at their table, and give you back a customer for life.
